The E-E-A-T + Medical Content Trust Checklist for Naturopathic Doctors

A 10-point authority audit. Make expertise visible, support health claims responsibly, and give patients clear reasons to trust the people and content behind every page.

Google's E-E-A-T framework — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — applies with extra weight to health and medical content. For naturopathic doctors, this means the content on your website needs to be traceable to a real, qualified person, supported by credible evidence, and maintained over time. This checklist covers the five areas where ND practices most often have gaps.

01

Identity

Make the practitioner unmistakable.

1

Show the practitioner's full identity and role

Patients should immediately know who is responsible for the care and content. Use a consistent professional name, designation, photograph, role, and practice affiliation.

  • Full name and ND designation are prominent
  • A current professional photograph is present
  • Role and areas of focus are described clearly
  • Practice or clinic affiliation is consistent

Quick test: Can a visitor identify the responsible practitioner in five seconds?

2

Make professional credentials easy to verify

List the education, registration, associations, and relevant experience that support the practitioner's expertise. Use precise facts rather than vague authority language.

  • Education and graduation school are listed
  • Provincial registration is named accurately
  • Relevant associations are current
  • Experience claims can be independently verified

Quick test: Could a cautious patient verify these credentials without guessing?

02

Accountability

Name who created and reviewed the content.

3

Add clear bylines and reviewer credits

Every substantial health article should identify its author. When clinical review adds value, name the reviewer and link both people to useful professional biographies.

  • Articles carry an accurate byline
  • Clinical reviewers are credited where appropriate
  • Names link to substantial author pages
  • Author expertise matches the subject

Quick test: Is it obvious who stands behind every health claim on the page?

4

Use created, reviewed, and updated dates honestly

Dates should tell readers what actually happened. Do not change an updated date unless the content received a meaningful factual or editorial review.

  • Original publication date is available
  • Clinical review date is shown when relevant
  • Updated date reflects a real change
  • Old or superseded advice is corrected

Quick test: Would the displayed date survive an editorial audit?

03

Evidence

Support claims without overpromising.

5

Cite credible sources close to important claims

Use authoritative guidance, systematic reviews, primary research, and recognized professional sources where appropriate. Make it easy to connect a claim to its support.

  • Important claims have nearby citations
  • Sources are authoritative for the topic
  • Links reach the original source when possible
  • Old evidence is reviewed for relevance

Quick test: Can a reader follow each important claim back to credible support?

6

Use responsible health and outcome language

Avoid guarantees, exaggerated certainty, fear, or universal claims. Acknowledge individual variation, appropriate referral boundaries, and what the practice does not promise.

  • No guaranteed outcomes are promised
  • Individual variation is acknowledged
  • Claims follow professional college rules
  • The page avoids diagnosing the reader

Quick test: Would the wording still feel accurate to a skeptical clinician?

04

Original Value

Add expertise that generic content cannot.

7

Explain who, how, and why behind the content

Make the author, editorial process, and purpose understandable. If automation materially shaped the work, disclose its role when readers would reasonably expect that context.

  • The author and reviewer are identifiable
  • The review process is described where useful
  • The page has a clear patient-first purpose
  • Material automation use is disclosed appropriately

Quick test: Can a reader understand who made this, how, and why?

8

Contribute original expert value

Go beyond generic summaries with useful explanations, careful examples, original frameworks, practical decision points, or clearly attributed professional perspective.

  • The page adds more than a search summary
  • Expert perspective is specific and useful
  • Examples protect patient privacy
  • The reader can take a safe next step

Quick test: Would someone bookmark this page even if search traffic disappeared?

05

Site-wide Trust

Carry trust through the whole website.

9

Keep practice identity and policies consistent

Use the same practice name, address, phone, practitioner details, and services across the website and key profiles. Make privacy, contact, and accessibility information easy to find.

  • Name, address, and phone stay consistent
  • Practitioner bios agree across the site
  • Privacy and contact pages are visible
  • Fees and appointment expectations are current

Quick test: Does every page describe the same real-world practice?

10

Schedule a recurring trust and accuracy review

Authority needs maintenance. Review high-impact health pages, credentials, sources, dates, policies, broken links, and professional claims on a documented schedule.

  • High-impact pages have named owners
  • A review schedule is documented
  • Broken citations and links are repaired
  • Material corrections are made promptly

Quick test: Who owns the next accuracy review, and when will it happen?

What your score means

0–3: Foundation missing. Resolve identity, accountability, and essential trust gaps first — these are what Google's quality raters look for first on health and medical pages.

4–6: Credible start. Choose the gaps with the greatest effect on patients and discovery — usually credentials, citations, and outcome language.

7–9: Strong authority. Close the remaining gaps and make a review schedule a standing item.

10/10: Ready to compound. Maintain the standard, measure outcomes, and keep improving.

Guidance used: Google People-first content guidelines; Google Generative AI content guidance. This checklist applies the E-E-A-T framework specifically to naturopathic practice websites.

Your first improvement sprint

Pick the single highest-impact gap from your score. Name who owns fixing it and set a target date. One resolved gap per month compounds quickly across a year.
