The 10-Out-of-10 Website Checklist for Naturopathic Doctors

After auditing dozens of ND websites, these are the 10 things that separate a site that quietly generates patients from one that sits there looking nice. Tick them off — or hand this to whoever built your site.

Most naturopath websites were built without a clear brief. A designer made something that looked professional. A developer put it online. The ND paid the invoice. And then… nothing. No new patients. No Google rankings. No momentum.

This checklist exists because "looks good" and "works well" are different things. A 10-out-of-10 website does both. Use it to score your current site, or use it as a brief for your next one.

out of 10

1

A clear booking CTA above the fold

Before a visitor scrolls, they should see a button that tells them exactly what to do next — "Book a free intro call," "Request an appointment," or "Check availability." Not a "Contact" link. A real button. If you use Jane App, the button should link directly to your booking page, not a contact form.

2

A headline that names who you help and where

Your H1 — the biggest text on the homepage — should answer: who is this for, what do they get, and where are you? "Naturopathic Care for Women's Health in Vancouver" works. "Welcome to Our Practice" does not. Specificity signals to both Google and patients that this page is for them.

3

A real photo of you — the doctor — on the homepage

Health is a high-trust purchase. Patients choose a person, not a logo. A professional photo of you — not a stock photo of a stethoscope, not a green leaf — should appear on the homepage, ideally above the fold or immediately below it. Your name and credentials (ND) should be prominently displayed nearby.

4

95+ on Google PageSpeed (mobile)

More than 60% of health searches happen on mobile. A slow site loses patients before they read a single word. Run your site through pagespeed.web.dev and check your mobile score. Anything below 80 is actively costing you patients. Below 50 is an emergency. Images should be WebP, fonts should preload, and there should be zero render-blocking scripts.

5

Local SEO built in from the start

Your page title should include "Naturopath [City]" or "Naturopathic Doctor [City]." Your city should appear in your H1, your first paragraph, and your page meta description. You should have a Google Business Profile that's fully complete and connected to your website. And you should have LocalBusiness structured data (JSON-LD) in your page head so Google can read your practice details directly.

6

Trust signals: testimonials, credentials, associations

Patients need to believe you can help them before they'll book. Display patient testimonials (with CNDA-compliant language — no specific outcome claims). Show your ND credential clearly. List professional associations: CAND, your provincial ND association, any specialty training. Show your clinic address and phone number — practices that hide their location feel less trustworthy. A Google review badge is a bonus.

7

Service pages — one per specialty

If you treat hormonal health, digestive issues, and anxiety, those should each have their own page — not a single "Services" page listing everything. Individual service pages rank for specific searches, build topical authority, and let patients see themselves in your content. A patient searching "naturopath for PCOS in Ottawa" should land on a page that speaks directly to that.

8

Optimised for AI search (AEO)

ChatGPT, Google's AI Overviews, and Perplexity are now answering health questions directly — and they cite specific sources. To be cited, your site needs clear, structured, authoritative content: FAQ sections with real questions patients ask, schema markup (FAQPage, MedicalOrganization), and content that directly answers "what does a naturopath treat?" and similar queries. This is the new SEO frontier.

9

A blog or content hub with at least 4 posts

Content is how your site grows over time. Every blog post is a new door into your site from Google. A minimum viable content strategy for NDs: one post per month, each targeting a specific question your patients ask ("can naturopathy help with sleep?" "what does a first naturopath appointment look like?"). Four posts is the minimum floor for Google to take your site seriously as an active resource.

10

Analytics + Google Search Console connected

You can't improve what you don't measure. Google Analytics (GA4) tells you how many visitors you're getting and where they come from. Google Search Console tells you which search terms are bringing people to your site and which pages are ranking. Both are free, both take under 30 minutes to set up, and both will tell you things about your site's performance that no audit ever could.

What your score means

0–3: Your site is likely costing you patients right now. The basics — fast load, clear CTA, local SEO — aren't in place. These are fixable within a few weeks.

4–6: You have a foundation. The next step is filling the gaps systematically. Focus on local SEO, service pages, and trust signals first — those have the highest direct ROI.

7–9: Your site is performing well above average for ND websites. The remaining items are likely AEO, analytics, or content depth. These compound over time.

10/10: Genuinely rare. If your site checks all of these, you're in the top percentile of naturopath websites in Canada. Keep publishing content and your position will only strengthen.
